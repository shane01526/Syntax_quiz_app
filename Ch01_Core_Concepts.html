本章是全書的方法論與哲學地基。Adger 在此回答一個看似簡單的問題——「什麼是句子？」——並藉此帶出生成語法的全部基本立場：句子是心理的、抽象的對象；母語者對句子的判斷（合語法性）是語言學的核心資料；這套句法知識是默會（tacit）、與生俱來的；語言學的任務是為這套知識建立一個形式化、可否證、最精簡的理論。最後用「遞迴」說明語言為何能以有限規則產生無限句子。

本章的問題意識

初學者常以為「句子」就是「印在紙上、念出聲音的一串字」。Adger 第 1 章要徹底翻轉這個直覺：句子不是那串聲音或字母（那叫「話語 utterance」），而是母語者腦中一個抽象的心理結構。語言學研究的對象，是這套讓人能判斷「這串字對不對」的內在知識系統（I-language），而非外在的言語表現。理解這個區分，是讀懂全書後續所有技術分析的前提。

1.1.1 話語、命題與句子

書頁 1

有些人類語言的話語似乎具有特殊地位——它們表達所謂的「完整思想」。例如我說「It's rather hot, today（今天相當熱）」，任何英語母語者都會理解成一個完整的訊息。Adger 用一個小場景帶出三個關鍵概念的區分：

概念英文定義
話語utterance句子的實際使用；外在、有物理形體（可錄音、可印刷、可輸入電腦）
命題proposition句子意義中可被判斷「真／假」的那一面；描述世界的某個狀態
句子sentence對「具相同形式的諸多話語」的抽象；內在、心理的實體，無物理形體

場景：我在星期日說「今天很熱」，你在星期一也說「今天很熱」——我們用了完全相同的句子（相同的語言形式），卻表達了不同的命題（我說的「今天」指週日，你的指週一，真假條件不同）。這顯示：同一句子可在不同場合表達不同命題；命題與語言形式無關。

(1) John saw Stephan.（英語）
(2) Jean a vu Stephan.（法語）
(3) Chunnaic Iain Stephan.（蘇格蘭蓋爾語） 這三句分屬不同語言、形式完全不同，卻表達同一個命題（John 看見了 Stephan）。這從另一個方向印證：命題獨立於語言形式。命題與形式是兩件可分離的事——這個區分是全書「句法（管形式）vs 語意（管命題）」分工的起點。
書頁 2

那麼「句子」本身如何定義？答案是：你我之所以能從一個話語讀出命題，是因為共享同一套語言知識，其中包括「如何組成英語句子」與「句子的命題如何依賴其形式」。句子是抽象的心理實體，無法被錄音或電子操作；能被操作的只有「句子的使用（話語）」。你眼前讀到的這些字，技術上其實是話語（有外在形體）；句子本身是內在、抽象的。

1.1.2 可接受性、合語法性與星號

本書主要關心句子的形式。同一命題可用不同形式表達——例如主動與被動：

(4) That monkey is eating the banana.
(5) The banana is being eaten by that monkey. (4) 主動、(5) 被動，形式不同卻傳達大致相同的訊息。呼應 1.1.1：一個命題可有多種形式。
書頁 3

反過來，多數人無法從 (6) 讀出任何訊息，會拒絕它作為英語句子：

(6) * By is eaten monkey banana that the being. 為何不可接受？因為詞序（word order）很重要。(6) 用的字和 (5) 一模一樣，但排列順序不是英語允許的。→ 顯示「順序」是句子合法與否的因素之一。
(7) * That monkey is ate the banana. 但可接受性不只取決於詞序。(7) 的詞序完全正常（和 (4) 一樣），壞的是詞的形式（form）——is 後面該接 eating（現在分詞）而非 ate（過去式）。→ 顯示「詞與詞之間的形式關係」也是因素。

還有些句子「怪」是因為與我們對世界的認知衝突，而非形式問題：

(8) The amoeba coughed.（變形蟲咳嗽了） 這句形式完全正常，但多數人覺得怪——因為在我們對世界的認知裡，變形蟲不會咳嗽。它的不可接受性取決於語境與世界觀（在奇幻小說或卡通裡就完全 OK）。→ 關鍵教訓：「不可接受」不等於「不合語法」；這裡是語意／世界知識的問題，不是句法問題。
書頁 3–4

另一種不可接受是「語意合理但彆扭」的句子，問題出在處理（剖析）：

(9) !I looked the number which you picked out at random by using a needle and a phonebook up.
(10) ✓ I looked up the number which you picked out…（up 緊接 look）
(11) ✓ I looked the number up.（look 與 up 距離短） (9) 中 look 與 up 之間隔了 15 個字，難以理解；把 up 拉近就變好 (10)(11)。Adger 假設：聽者處理時，遇到 look 就預期 up，但等太久、中間又要處理複雜結構，連結就難建立。→ 這是 剖析解釋（parsing explanation）：問題不在「不合文法」，而在人腦處理時難以指派正確結構，極端時甚至無法剖析（unparsable）。

核心區分：剖析解釋 vs 合語法性解釋

(9) 可以靠「縮短距離」變好 → 屬剖析（performance/表現）問題。但 (7) 無法靠縮短距離變好——語言根本就要求 is 後面接特定形式，這個關係在 (7) 裡就是不成立。這種解釋叫 合語法性解釋（grammaticality explanation），這類句子稱不合語法（ungrammatical）。身為語言學家，我們無法事先知道一個句子的不可接受是因為難剖析、不合語法、還是其他因素——這是判斷時必須小心的。

句法學家用星號 * 標記「這串字不是該語言的合語法句子」：

(12) * By is eaten monkey banana that the being.
(13) * The monkey is ate the banana. 一串字稱為 string（字串）；在字串前加星號 = 主張它不是該語言的合語法句子。這個 * 慣例會貫穿全書。
書頁 5

星號還用於「在特定解讀下不合法」——句子形式沒問題，但無法承載某個指定的意義：

(14) People are in the garden.（存在解讀：有些人在花園裡）
(15) People are stupid.（通則解讀：人（普遍而言）是愚蠢的） (14) 只能是「存在」解讀（有些人在花園），不能是「人普遍都在花園」；(15) 反過來只能是「通則」解讀（人都笨），不能是「存在些笨人」。所以 (15) 在「存在解讀」下被加星（starred under that interpretation）。
(16) How did Julie ask if Jenny left?
(17) How did Julie say that Jenny left? (16) 只能問「Julie 問的方式」（大聲、無禮…），不能問「Jenny 離開的方式」。但 (17) 卻可以問「Jenny 離開的方式」（生氣地、輕快地…）。所以 (16) 在「問 Jenny 離開方式」這個解讀下不合語法。為何？因為英語的句法知識不允許把 (16) 的詞組成能得出該意義的結構——這預告了第 9、10 章的「wh-移位」與「島嶼」限制。
書頁 6

為何 * 同時用於「不是句子」與「沒有預期的意義」兩種情況？因為命題不只取決於有哪些字，也取決於字如何組合。母語者能給 (17) 指派兩種結構、卻只能給 (16) 指派一種；缺少的那種結構就用 * 標記（並在上下文註明所指的解讀）。

1.1.3 形式與順序

回到更明顯的形式事實。詞序會改變命題：

(18) That banana is eating the monkey.（那根香蕉在吃猴子） 與 (4) 用完全相同的字，但詞序不同 → 意思相反。再次證明詞序攸關命題。

詞與詞之間還有形式上的牽連。(18) 的第一、三個字（that…is）的形式由第二個字決定；改變第二字，第一、三字也得跟著改：

(19) Those monkeys are eating the banana.
(20) * Those monkey are eating the banana.
(21) * That monkeys is eating the banana. 不能把 are 換成 is（或反之）：those↔monkeys↔are 必須一致，that↔monkey↔is 必須一致。這種「一個詞牽動其他詞形式」的關係稱 一致（agreement）（後續章節詳論）。它無法用剖析解釋——純粹是結構問題。
書頁 7

意義也是母語者有強烈直覺的對象。最明顯的是 歧義（ambiguous）：

(22) The monkey wrote a poem on its favourite banana. 兩個意思：① 猴子寫了一首「關於」它最愛的香蕉的詩（write about）；② 猴子真的把詩寫「在」香蕉表面上（write on the surface）。同一串字、同一詞序，卻有兩個意義 → 顯示句子有結構，而結構規定了成分之間的關係。
本節結論：句子不只是「字一個接一個串起來」，而是有句法結構（syntactic structure）——它規定了句子各片段之間成立哪些關係。句法學家的主要任務就是：找出語言的基本構件，以及這些構件之間有哪些關係。

1.2 默會知識

書頁 7–8

上一節說的「知識」是語言學的技術義，與「歷史知識」截然不同。Adger 用四個對比說明語言知識的特殊性：

歷史知識語言知識
片面的（partial）：只知道片段近乎完整：你很難教我一個英語沒有的造句法
易忘的（forgettable）：會忘記日期不會「忘記如何造句」（除非嚴重腦傷）
外顯的（explicit）：能說出口無法用意識說清楚造句規則
學來的（learned）：刻意學習近乎本能、無須教導
(23) ✓ Anson is incredibly difficult to please.
(24) * Anson is incredibly difficult to be pleased. 母語者立刻知道 (23) 是英語句子、(24) 不是——但說不出為什麼 (24) 這麼糟。這種「知道卻說不出所以然」的知識，正是 默會知識（tacit knowledge）。本書的科學任務就是把這套說不出口的知識，用明確的理論揭露出來。
書頁 8–9

語言習得的特殊性。多數兒童三歲就掌握母語核心結構。三歲兩個月的孩子能說出 (25)(26)——雖有一致錯誤（I are、a carpenter keep），卻已展現「組合句子並操作其部件」的複雜結構。

刺激貧乏論（Poverty of the Stimulus）——本章最重要的論證

以英語 yes/no 問句為例。問句 (27) Has Jenny eaten a cake? / (28) Will Anson come to the party? 顯然對應於陳述句 (29) Jenny has eaten a cake / (30) Anson will come。孩子聽到問句後，理論上可以「夢想出」(31) 多條規則來生成它：

  • 規則1：把前兩個字對調。
  • 規則2：把第一個動詞性成分與第一個名詞片語對調。
  • 規則3：把主語之後的動詞性成分移到主語之前。（正確規則）
書頁 9–10

三條規則在簡單句上結果相同，但在複雜句（主語含關係子句）上會分歧。若孩子用規則1（對調前兩字），會產生 (32b) *Man the has eaten the cake、(33b) *Woman the who is singing is happy——顯然錯。若用規則2，會產生 (35b) *Is the woman who singing is happy——也錯。正確的規則3 產生 (37b) Is the woman who is singing happy?

(35b) * Is the woman who singing is happy?（規則2 的錯誤預測）
(37b) ✓ Is the woman who is singing happy?（規則3，正確） Crain & Nakayama (1987) 的實驗顯示：連三歲兩個月的孩子，都不犯規則1、2 預測的錯誤，一律用最複雜的規則3。關鍵在於：規則3 必須認識到句子有階層結構（要先找出「主語」這個結構單位），而非單純的線性順序。而孩子聽到的語料根本不足以排除規則1、2——他們卻天生就知道要用規則3。這就是刺激貧乏論：兒童擁有的語言知識，不可能單從他們聽到的輸入學來。
書頁 10–12

為何把這稱為「知識」？源自柏拉圖之問——我們如何能用如此少的輸入知道如此多？Chomsky 稱之為 柏拉圖問題（Plato's Problem）。其他重要概念：

  • 認知（cognize）：若哲學家堅持「知識」須是「真確且有理據的信念」，語言學家願改用 cognize 一詞——但「（默會）知識」是合理的技術術語。
  • grammar（文法）有兩義要小心：(i) 說話者對其語言的默會知識；(ii) 語言學家對該默會知識的外顯理論。
  • I-language（I = Internal/Individual，內在／個體）：Chomsky 對「文法第一義」的稱呼，即說話者的默會語言知識。語言學家建構的是「關於 I-language 的理論」。
  • I-language 有生理基礎：左腦的 Broca's area 與 Wernicke's area 受損會造成特定語言缺陷；特定型語言障礙（SLI）有遺傳因素（已找到相關基因突變）；但習得哪種語言由環境決定。

I-language 是人類心智中把意義（部分為命題）與形式（聲音、字母、手語符號）連結起來的部件。它高度創造性，能把無限的意義連到無限的符號序列——這是人類溝通、乃至物種演化成功的根本。

1.3 句法理論

書頁 12–13

兩個已得結論：(i) 句子是抽象心理對象；(ii) 一個句子是否屬於某人的母語，是該說話者默會地知道的。據此可初步定義：句法知識（syntax）是 I-language 中關於「句子如何由基本部件組合而成」的那部分——這正是我們要建構理論的對象。

什麼是理論？理論是一組能解釋某些現象的普遍原則／定律。一個理論要考慮兩件事：(1) 它的領域（domain）（要解釋什麼現象）；(2) 它能做出哪些陳述（陳述關於什麼、允許哪些關係）。重要的是：我們無法在研究之前就知道陳述該關於什麼——理論是逐步發展、再探索其後果的。成功的理論不僅解釋已觀察的現象，還能對新情況做出可檢驗的預測，且預測錯誤時理論可被挑戰。

書頁 14

形式化（formal）是物理、化學、生物等成功理論的特徵：系統是明確的、概念有良好定義。明確定義的好處是更難被誤用。生成語法假設：人類心理的某些面向（包括語言結構）類似自然界現象——這由腦的物理結構、人類的遺傳結構都涉入語言結構而得到支持。因此，那些在處理自然界如此成功的理論手法，很可能有助於理解語言。這也呼應「在自然中尋找數學模式」的 伽利略取向（Galilean approach）：語言中的數學模式是什麼？

書頁 15

精簡原則。理論應盡量精簡——盡量少假設概念與關係（源自 William of Ockham 的 奧坎剃刀（Ockham's Razor））。理論的基礎要簡單，即使其效應可能很複雜。句法理論要陳述：基本單位是什麼（句法原子／形符 syntactic primes / formatives），以及這些單位如何彼此關聯（句法關係 syntactic relations）。有了理論，就對某組資料做分析（analysis）。

理論的三級「充分性（adequacy）」：
① 觀察充分性（observational adequacy）：只要「把對的字按對的順序排出來」即可（最低標準）。
② 描述充分性（descriptive adequacy）：要能對句子指派正確的結構，描述語言裡所有結構及其互動（更嚴格）。
③ 解釋充分性（explanatory adequacy）：要能解釋兒童為何在給定輸入下習得「這一個」I-language、而非其他同樣相容於資料卻做錯預測的 I-language（最高標準）。
書頁 16

所有人共享的 I-language 面向稱 普遍語法（Universal Grammar, UG）。UG 理論陳述：跨語言共通的部分（UG 原則 Principles），以及各語言可分歧之處（變異參數 Parameters）。例如 UG 可能要求所有語言都有「動詞與主語的一致關係」，但某些語言不發音、或一致也及於賓語。參數少 → 可能的分析少 → 能解釋兒童為何能在貧乏資料下如此快速習得語言（解釋充分性）。

1.4 回到句子：遞迴與離散無限性

書頁 16–17

I-language 的核心性質之一：它刻畫潛在無限多的句子。我們日常用的許多句子都是初次說出的新句，卻毫無困難。怎麼可能？難道大腦把所有句子都存起來？不可能。

(38) Paul had an affair.
(39)–(42) Paul had two / three / … eighty-eight billion … affairs. 光是改數字就能無限造句——句子數遠超 Paul 的風流韻事。但更根本的證明來自內嵌（embedding）。
(43) Anson thought Julie had fainted.
(44) You said that [Anson thought that Julie had fainted].
(45) David wrote that [you said that Anson thought that Julie had fainted]. 每一次把整個句子內嵌進另一個句子，就得到一個新句子；這個過程原則上永無止境（實際上會因疲倦或死亡而停）。因此句子數是無限的。這也帶出 能力（competence，＝I-language，原則上能做什麼） 與 表現（performance，實際使用） 的區分——剖析是表現問題，合語法性是能力問題。
書頁 18–19

這種創造力絕對依賴句子有結構。一個明確而精簡的陳述方式是 詞組結構規則（Phrase Structure Rules, PS-rules）（早期生成語法用、後已被取代；可想成「把箭頭左邊的符號改寫成右邊的東西」）：

(46) S → Paul had an affair.
(47) S → You said that S 注意 (47) 箭頭兩邊都有 S。套用 (47) 的輸出，又可作為 (47) 的新輸入——例如推導 (49)：S → You said that S → You said that you said that S → … → You said that you said that Paul had an affair。
遞迴（recursion）：像 (47) 這樣「同一符號出現在箭頭兩側」、能不斷回頭套用自己的規則，稱為 遞迴規則；由此產生的語言是 遞迴語言。正是「人類語言是遞迴的」這個性質，讓有限的規則能生成（generate）無限的句子——這就是我們在語言裡找到的數學模式。我們希望用盡量精簡的理論來捕捉這個根本性質（第 3 章詳論）。

p.1

1.1 What is a sentence? — 1.1.1 Utterances, propositions, and sentences. Certain utterances of human languages seem to have a special status, in that they express what you might call 'complete thoughts'. So if I were to say, "It's rather hot, today", any native English speaker will interpret me as conveying a message which is somehow complete. Note that you could say exactly the same thing, "It's rather hot, today", which will also convey a complete message. However, if I do this on Sunday, and you do it on Monday, the message communicated is different. Linguists say that the proposition expressed is different.

A proposition is that aspect of the meaning of a sentence which allows us to say, "Yes, that's true" or "No, that's false". It describes a state of affairs that holds in the world, and its correspondence with that state of affairs allows us to attribute truth or falsity to the proposition.

Note that, even though we have expressed different propositions, we have both used exactly the same linguistic form. We have both said the same sentence. This little scenario gives us some grasp of a core idea in syntax, the idea of a sentence as an abstraction over utterances which have the same form. The same proposition is conveyed by the English sentence (1), the French sentence (2), and the Scottish Gaelic sentence (3):

(1) John saw Stephan.
(2) Jean a vu Stephan.
(3) Chunnaic Iain Stephan.
p.2

So a proposition is that aspect of the meaning of a sentence which says something about a state of affairs, and an utterance is an actual use of a sentence. A sentence itself is something which can't be recorded, heard, or electronically manipulated, only uses of sentences can. Although it sounds counter-intuitive, what you see on this page are technically utterances. Sentences, on the other hand, are internal, mental entities, which have an abstract form.

1.1.2 Acceptability, grammaticality, and stars. We can express the same proposition using different sentence forms:

(4) That monkey is eating the banana.
(5) The banana is being eaten by that monkey.
p.3
(6) *By is eaten monkey banana that the being.

Why is (6) not an acceptable sentence of English? Well obviously the order of the words matters. Acceptability, however, doesn't just depend upon the order of words.

(7) *That monkey is ate the banana.

It's the form of the words that makes the sentence unacceptable; the word after is should have the form eating, not ate.

(8) The amoeba coughed.

The acceptability of this sentence depends upon the context. The acceptability of (8) appears to be dependent on our view of the world.

(9) !I looked the number which you picked out at random by using a needle and a phonebook up.
(10) I looked up the number which you picked out at random…
p.4
(11) I looked the number up.

This kind of explanation for unacceptability is known as a parsing explanation. In the most extreme cases we might conclude that the sentence can't be parsed at all—it is unparsable. This kind of explanation for the unacceptability of (7) is known as a grammaticality explanation, and sentences like (7) are termed ungrammatical. Syntacticians express the claim with a star:

(12) *By is eaten monkey banana that the being.
(13) *The monkey is ate the banana.

Sometimes a sequence of words is called a string.

p.5

Acceptability concerns making a judgement about the status of a string as part of a language. As linguists, we cannot know in advance whether a string of words is unacceptable because it is difficult to parse, because it is ungrammatical, or because of some other factor.

(14) People are in the garden.
(15) People are stupid.
(16) How did Julie ask if Jenny left?
(17) How did Julie say that Jenny left?

This is not a possible meaning of (16). In that case, we would say that (16) is ungrammatical under this interpretation.

p.6

The propositions expressed by sentences are not just dependent on the words in a sentence, but also on the ways that those words are put together. 1.1.3 Form and order.

(18) That banana is eating the monkey.
(19) Those monkeys are eating the banana.
(20) *Those monkey are eating the banana.
(21) *That monkeys is eating the banana.
p.7

The relationship between the second word and the others is known as agreement. The meanings of sentences are also things which native speakers have strong intuitions about; (22) has two meanings (i.e. it is ambiguous):

(22) The monkey wrote a poem on its favourite banana.

Sentences have a structure which specifies certain relationships. The general term is the syntactic structure of the sentence. 1.2 Tacit knowledge. Knowledge of language isn't like knowledge of history. My knowledge of history is extremely partial, forgettable, explicit, and learned.

p.8
(23) Anson is incredibly difficult to please.
(24) *Anson is incredibly difficult to be pleased.

The kind of knowledge of language which allows you to make this judgement is not accessible to you—the term for this is tacit knowledge. Most children have a command over the core structures of their language by the time they are three (Pinker 1994, ch. 7):

(25) Can I put my head in the mailbox so the mailman can know where I are and put me in the mailbox?
p.9
(26) Can I keep the screwdriver just like a carpenter keep the screwdriver?
(27) Has Jenny eaten a cake?
(28) Will Anson come to the party?
(29) Jenny has eaten a cake.
(30) Anson will come to the party.
(31) 1. Swap the first two words around. 2. Swap the first verbal element with the first noun phrase. 3. Put the verbal element that follows the subject in front of it.
(32) a. The man has eaten the cake. b. *Man the has eaten the cake.
(33) a. The woman who is singing is happy. b. *Woman the who is singing is happy.
p.10
(34) a. The man has eaten the cake. b. Has the man eaten the cake?
(35) a. The woman who is singing is happy. b. *Is the woman who singing is happy.
(36) b. Has the man eaten the cake?
(37) b. Is the woman who is singing happy?

Crain and Nakayama (1987) showed that the errors expected under the first two hypotheses never occurred. This kind of argument is known as a Poverty of the Stimulus argument: the linguistic stimulus that a child is exposed to is not rich enough to provide the information the child needs to acquire the language. In many senses, this tacit knowledge is far more like instinct than other kinds of knowledge.

p.11

The answer has to do with a discussion that goes back to Plato—a problem which Chomsky has christened Plato's Problem. The linguists' use of knowledge (and especially tacit knowledge) is a technical term (cf. cognize). Another common term is grammar: (i) a speaker's tacit knowledge; (ii) a linguist's explicit theory of it. Chomsky refers to the first sense as I-language (I = Internal/Individual). Two areas of the brain's left hemisphere: Broca's area and Wernicke's area. Some aspects are genetically determined—Specific Language Impairment (SLI).

p.12

Other aspects of I-language are determined by environmental factors. I-language allows us to link together meanings with forms, and is highly creative. Syntax is key to human achievement at a fundamental level. 1.3 Syntactic theories. Syntactic knowledge is that part of an I-language that is concerned with the ways that sentences are put together out of basic parts.

p.13

A theory is a statement of general principles or laws. We don't know what the statements are about in advance of investigation. A successful theory engenders new questions, gives explanations of observed phenomena, and makes testable predictions.

p.14

Theories of physics, genetics, etc. have a formal aspect. The approach in generative grammar assumes that certain aspects of human psychology are similar to phenomena of the natural world. This relates to the search for "mathematical patterns in nature" — the Galilean approach.

p.15

We keep theories as simple as possible (Ockham's Razor). The basic units are syntactic primes/formatives; how they relate are syntactic relations; we then develop an analysis. Levels: observational adequacy ('right words in the right order'); descriptive adequacy (assign the right structures); explanatory adequacy (explain how a child fixes on the right I-language).

p.16

The aspects common to all of us are Universal Grammar (UG): Principles and Parameters. Few parameters → few analyses → explains rapid acquisition. 1.4 Back to sentences.

(38) Paul had an affair.
p.17
(39) Paul had two affairs. (40) Paul had three affairs… (41)/(42) Paul had eighty-eight billion … affairs…
(43) Anson thought Julie had fainted.
(44) You said that Anson thought that Julie had fainted.
(45) David wrote that you said that Anson thought that Julie had fainted.

We can take a sentence and embed it inside another, indefinitely → an infinite number of sentences. The distinction between competence (= I-language) and performance (putting it to use). Parsing is performance; grammaticality is competence.

p.18

The creativity of I-languages depends on the fact that sentences have structure. A general, explicit, minimal statement: Phrase Structure Rules (PS-rules).

(46) S → Paul had an affair.
(47) S → You said that S
(48) a. S (apply 46) b. Paul had an affair.
(49) a. S (apply 47) b. You said that S (apply 47) c. You said that you said that S (apply 46) d. You said that you said that Paul had an affair.
p.19

The rules give rise to (technically generate) an infinite number of sentences. Sets of rules which refer back to themselves are called recursive. It is partly the fact that human languages are recursive that allows them to be creative. It seems that we have found a mathematical pattern in language.

p.19–21

1.5 Summary. We distinguished semantic and physical properties of a sentence from its structural properties via proposition and utterance. Human beings assign structure to a string via tacit knowledge of language (I-language), motivated by judgements of parsability, semantic well-formedness, and structural well-formedness. We build a theory that is formal, general, and maximally simple, that explains Poverty of the Stimulus facts and both linguistic universals and variation (basic principles + parameters), and that accounts for the recursive nature of human languages.
